Notes on Therapy #1: Taking Ideas Seriously

The effects of therapy have an annoying timeline. After a session, things are often better. I don’t feel so bad anymore. I am more in control of my life. This carries on for another day or two and then degrades. The depressive ruminations begin once more.

I noticed this cycle two months into my therapy. Before I went in to speak with my therapist, I was pretty miserable, low mood, and couldn’t get around too much. Afterwards, I was pumped, smiling more, and ready to take on life. People’s thoughts bothered me less. The troubles of the universe weren’t scary; “life is a gift” and other tunes were sung in my head. Eventually, the songs became less joyful and more irritating. They’d start screeching.

This cycle is frustrating. I think this frustration made me feel less positively about therapy. What would the point be if I was going to feel miserable a few days later?

I told a close friend about this, and they told me to stick with it. It’s hard for me to stick with something that feels futile, but I have a lot of faith in my friend, and so I trusted them. Instead of giving up on therapy entirely, I tried to understand why this cycle was happening.

I don’t think I’ve found the root cause of the cycle, but I’ve found a way to slow it down. This has come from realising that I was passively taking in the ideas from therapy. I wasn’t acting on them. I’ve been told several times that what matters is how I perceive myself. Other people’s opinions don’t matter. I nodded in agreement every time this was said, but I didn’t believe it. I grew up with the opposite being told to me, and this worked its way towards being deeply ingrained in my psyche.

What was missing was an intention to take the advice from therapy seriously. I needed to adopt the identity of someone who believes in what their therapist says.

This seems to have worked out brilliantly. I feel like I am active, not passive anymore. I don’t know if the cycle I was stuck in has disappeared, but it has halted for a bit and helped restore my faith in this whole therapy thing.
